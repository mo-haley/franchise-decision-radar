# Cohort Thesis Memo — Mosquito Pest Control Franchises (2025 FDDs)

**Classification:** Internal analytical guidance. Not for publication.
**Date:** 2026-03-14
**Source:** 2025 FDDs filed with Wisconsin DFI for all 5 brands.

---

## Executive Summary

Five PE-backed mosquito/pest control franchise brands compete for the same
prospective buyer pool. All are in the $50K–$200K initial investment range, all
operate seasonal barrier spray businesses, and all charge royalties in the 8–10%
range. Despite surface similarity, the cohort reveals sharp differences in
disclosure quality, system health, fee burden, and risk profile.

**The single most important finding:** The two fastest-growing brands by net unit
count (Mosquito Shield and Mosquito Joe) are also the two with the most concerning
churn signals. Growth alone is not a health indicator in this cohort.

---

## 1. Most Decision-Relevant Differences

### Cost of Entry
The cohort spans a 3.5x range in initial investment. Mosquito Authority's low end
($54K) is genuinely accessible; Mosquito Squad's high end ($220K) reflects a
premium positioning — though $84K–$117K of that is 12-month working capital
reserves, not franchise fees. Adjusting Squad to 3-month reserves brings it to
roughly $105K–$135K, mid-cohort.

Mosquito Hunters is the outlier on franchise fee: $107K ($50K license + $57K
mandatory training/supply), the highest in the cohort by far, for the smallest
and historically most troubled system.

### Ongoing Fee Burden
This is where the cohort diverges most sharply and where buyers are most likely
to be surprised post-purchase.

- **Mosquito Authority** has the lightest ongoing marketing burden: $5,500/year
  minimum local ad spend, no active national fund. Technology fee is $100–$1,200/month
  (tiered by revenue). Total ongoing burden is the lowest in the cohort.

- **Mosquito Joe** has the heaviest: $37K/year mandatory Direct Marketing Program +
  $35K/year local marketing + 2% MAP fee + $325/month SEO = ~$75K+ in marketing
  alone before a single customer is acquired. This is roughly 15–25% of first-year
  gross revenue for a typical new franchisee.

- **Mosquito Shield** has a $50K/year local ad minimum — highest single local ad
  floor in the cohort. Combined with 2% brand fund and mandatory bookkeeping
  ($200–$500/month), ongoing costs are high.

- **Mosquito Hunters** requires the greater of $37,500 or 10% of Net Revenues for
  marketing. For a sub-$375K revenue unit, the dollar floor dominates, making
  marketing burden regressive.

- **Mosquito Squad** has the most balanced structure: flat brand fund ($150–$450/month
  escalating), local marketing capped at $50K with a $35K floor, and a $350/month
  website fee. Total is material (~$40K–$55K/year) but has a ceiling.

### System Health Trajectory
Three brands show improving trajectories (Authority, Hunters, Squad). Two show
deteriorating signals (Joe, Shield) despite headline growth.

### Disclosure Rigor
Mosquito Squad provides dramatically more Item 19 data than any competitor.
Mosquito Hunters provides the least useful Item 19 in the cohort. This difference
alone should matter to a sophisticated buyer — a franchisor that provides close
rates, renewal rates, per-appointment revenue, same-store growth, and a full
company-owned P&L is signaling confidence. One that provides only per-treatment
pricing is either unable or unwilling to disclose unit economics.

## 2. Disclosure Quality Assessment

### Strongest Disclosure: Mosquito Squad
Seven Item 19 tables. Full company-owned P&L at meaningful scale ($20.8M, 14
territories). Only brand providing close rates, renewal rates, same-store growth,
and per-appointment revenue. Only document quality issue is a table numbering error.
This is the FDD a buyer should read first to calibrate expectations for the category.

### Strong: Mosquito Authority
Rich Item 19 with full company-owned P&L and detailed franchised revenue
distributions. The `#REF!` spreadsheet error and minor population count mismatches
are concerning but not disqualifying — they suggest automation issues, not deception.
The predecessor regulatory history (5 state actions for selling without registration)
is a real mark against the brand's heritage, though the current entity appears clean.

### Adequate: Mosquito Joe
Useful tenure-split gross sales data and strong retention metrics. However, three
tables have wrong column headers (copy errors in a filed regulatory document),
and there is no company-owned P&L despite the franchisor owning 2 locations.
The 2024 termination spike (24, up from 5) is not explained anywhere in the FDD.

### Below Average: Mosquito Shield
The 21% Item 19 exclusion rate is the most concerning data quality issue in the
cohort. "Non-conforming" is not defined. Average gross sales ($285,839) are
materially inflated by excluding the weakest fifth of the system. The company-owned
P&L provides only percentages, not dollar amounts. The one-unit Table 1 vs Table 3
discrepancy is minor but adds to a pattern of imprecision. Minimum Gross Sales
requirements with shortfall penalties are aggressive.

### Weakest Disclosure: Mosquito Hunters
No total revenue per franchise unit. No cost data. No P&L data. Item 19 consists
of per-treatment pricing and per-customer value by region — useful for pricing
benchmarking but useless for assessing unit economics. 26% of Strategic-Partners
excluded, including 8 for "failing to invest in MH marketing programs" (circular:
exclude those who don't spend on marketing, then report metrics for those who do).
The "Special Risk" flag on page 4 of the FDD is unusual and noteworthy.

## 3. Red Flags by Brand

### Mosquito Shield — High Growth Masking High Churn
- 65 terminations in 2023, 44 in 2024 (highest absolute numbers in cohort)
- Simultaneously opened 103 and 73 new outlets — net growth masks underlying attrition
- Turnover rate hit 24.7% in 2023 (1 in 4 outlets experienced some form of churn)
- 21% Item 19 exclusion rate inflates reported performance
- $50K/year local ad minimum is the highest mandatory marketing floor
- Minimum Gross Sales thresholds with punitive 7%-of-shortfall penalty
- Massachusetts concentration: +25 outlets in one state in one year

**Assessment:** Mosquito Shield is growing fast but churning hard. A buyer must ask:
are terminations concentrated in recent (cheap-to-open) franchisees? The combination
of rapid franchise sales + high terminations + high marketing floors + performance
minimums + vague Item 19 exclusions is the most concerning pattern in the cohort.

### Mosquito Joe — Termination Spike in a Mature System
- 24 terminations in 2024, up from 5 in 2023 (5x increase)
- Alabama went from 9 outlets to 1 (8 terminations in one state)
- System contracted for the first time (-1 net units)
- $75K+ first-year marketing spend is the highest in the cohort
- Franchisor litigated a franchisee over mandatory marketing fee non-payment
- 6-layer SPV/securitization entity structure

**Assessment:** Mosquito Joe has the strongest brand recognition (KKR/Neighborly) but
the most expensive ongoing cost structure and a sudden, unexplained termination
spike. The Alabama cluster suggests either market failure, enforcement action, or a
single multi-unit operator exit. The litigation over DMP fees confirms the franchisor
will enforce the $37K/year marketing program aggressively.

### Mosquito Hunters — Turnaround in Progress, Limited Visibility
- System contracted for 2 consecutive years (-4 in 2022, -1 in 2023) before
  rebounding (+13 in 2024)
- All closures coded exclusively as "terminations" — zero non-renewals, zero ceased
  operations across all 3 years, which is statistically implausible and suggests the
  franchisor does not distinguish between closure types
- $107K franchise fee is the highest in the cohort for the smallest system
- $37,500 marketing floor on a system where many units may gross under $375K
- Weakest Item 19: no revenue-per-unit disclosure at all
- "Special Risk" designation on FDD page 4

**Assessment:** The 2024 turnaround (zero terminations, +13 net) is a genuinely
positive signal, and the holiday lighting diversification is interesting. But the
buyer is flying blind on unit economics — no revenue data, no P&L, no cost data.
The high franchise fee relative to system size and disclosure quality is hard to
justify unless the combination franchise model (pest + mosquito + holiday lighting)
creates materially higher revenue per unit than competitors. That hypothesis cannot
be evaluated because the data is not disclosed.

## 4. "Cheap Entry" Risk Analysis

### Mosquito Authority ($54K–$128K)
The lowest-cost entry in the cohort. But is cheap hiding risk?

**Mitigating factors:**
- Largest system (546 units), 16-year track record
- Zero terminations across 3 years
- Rich Item 19 disclosure
- Fee deferral option (50% of franchise fee at 8%)
- Lightest ongoing marketing burden

**Risk factors:**
- Predecessor (TMA) had 5 state regulatory actions for selling without registration
- National Marketing Fee reserved but not yet implemented (could add 3% overnight)
- Company-owned location runs at negative net income after amortization
- Tiered technology fee ($100–$1,200/month) adds unpredictable ongoing cost

**Assessment:** Mosquito Authority is genuinely the lowest-risk entry point in the
cohort. The cheap price reflects a lean, home-based business model, not hidden costs.
The predecessor regulatory history is real but has been clean under current ownership
since 2020. The biggest risk is the reserved-but-unimplemented national marketing fee.

### Mosquito Shield ($120K–$158K)
Mid-range entry cost, but ongoing costs are high.

**Hidden costs:**
- $50K/year local ad minimum kicks in after year 1
- $200–$500/month mandatory bookkeeping vendor
- $300–$750/month sales center fee
- Minimum Gross Sales thresholds (franchisor can collect 7% of shortfall)

**Assessment:** Mosquito Shield's initial investment understates true first-year
costs. The $50K ad minimum alone is $14K higher than Mosquito Squad's floor.
Combined with mandatory vendor fees, a Shield franchisee faces $55K–$65K in
non-discretionary annual costs beyond royalties.

## 5. "Premium" Justification Analysis

### Mosquito Squad ($162K–$220K)
Highest headline investment. Is it justified?

**Arguments for:**
- Highest average revenue per territory ($484,506) in the cohort
- Only brand with close rates (48%), renewal rates (70%), same-store growth (5%)
- Company-owned operation proves 25.9% net margins at scale
- Brand Fund is a flat fee (not percentage) — caps marketing cost as you grow
- Local marketing has a ceiling ($50K) — unlike Shield's uncapped 10%
- 12-month reserves in Item 7 are conservative/honest, not inflated pricing
- Systemwide revenue ($124.3M) is 5x the next closest brand

**Arguments against:**
- System contracted in 2022 (-10 net units) before recovering
- Only 226 franchised territories (smallest PE-backed system after Hunters)
- MA Attorney General AOD limits marketing language
- Aggressive termination penalty ($50K+ liquidated damages)
- Minimum royalty escalates to $3,000/month by year 9+

**Assessment:** Mosquito Squad is the strongest economic performer in the cohort by
a significant margin, with the most transparent disclosure. The premium pricing
is defensible if a buyer values disclosed unit economics, system maturity, and
operational benchmarks. It is also the franchise with the most mature and stabilized
system — which means less territory availability.

### Mosquito Joe ($150K–$192K)
Second-highest investment. Is it justified?

**Arguments for:**
- Largest brand portfolio (Neighborly/KKR), strongest consumer recognition
- 77% customer retention, 89% recurring
- Tiered royalty rewards growth (drops to 7% above $500K)
- Second-largest system (415 units)

**Arguments against:**
- $75K+ first-year marketing is the highest in the cohort
- 2024 termination spike is unexplained
- 6-layer SPV structure adds counterparty complexity
- Will litigate franchisees over marketing fee disputes

**Assessment:** Mosquito Joe's brand value is real, but the cost to access it is
the highest in the cohort when ongoing fees are included. The 2024 termination
spike casts doubt on the system's health trajectory at precisely the moment a
buyer would need confidence. A prospective buyer should demand a clear explanation
for the Alabama termination cluster before proceeding.

## 6. Emerging Patterns

### Pattern 1: Growth Quality > Growth Quantity
Mosquito Shield's 3-year net growth (+140) looks strongest on paper. But its 3-year
gross attrition (128 terminations + ceased operations) is also highest. Net growth
of +140 required 267 openings against 128 losses. Compare to Mosquito Authority:
net growth of +22 from 39 openings against 17 losses. Authority's growth is "cleaner"
— fewer units entering and leaving the system.

### Pattern 2: Marketing Burden Is the Key Hidden Cost
The most significant financial difference between brands is not the franchise fee or
royalty rate — it's the mandatory marketing spend. Range: $5,500/year (Authority) to
$75,000+/year (Joe). This is the variable most likely to determine first-year
profitability and is the least transparent during the sales process.

### Pattern 3: Disclosure Rigor Correlates with System Maturity
The two oldest systems (Authority, Squad — both 16 years) provide the richest Item 19
data. The two youngest (Shield 12 years, Hunters 10 years) provide the weakest. This
may reflect institutional maturity or a deliberate strategy by newer systems to limit
buyer leverage during negotiation.

### Pattern 4: Per-Customer Economics Are Surprisingly Consistent
Despite different brand positioning, territory sizes, and geographic mixes, the
per-customer annual value converges around $700–$730 across the three brands that
report it (Hunters R2: $726, Shield: $715, Squad: $705). This suggests the
underlying service economics of mosquito barrier spray are fundamentally similar
across brands, and that the real differentiators are customer acquisition cost,
retention, and operational efficiency — not pricing power.

### Pattern 5: All Five Are PE-Backed, Creating Structural Similarities
Every brand in this cohort is PE-backed. This means:
- All are optimized for franchise unit sales as a growth metric
- All face pressure to demonstrate AUM growth to PE sponsors
- All have complex entity structures (SPVs, holdcos, assetcos)
- Exit risk exists for all if PE sponsors change strategy

There is no founder-operated or bootstrapped alternative in this cohort.

## 7. Summary Ranking (Internal Use Only)

**By confidence in making a well-informed buying decision:**
1. Mosquito Squad — Best data, strongest economics, most transparent
2. Mosquito Authority — Best cost/risk ratio, rich disclosure, lowest burden
3. Mosquito Joe — Strong brand but expensive and showing stress
4. Mosquito Shield — Fast growth masking high churn, weak disclosure
5. Mosquito Hunters — Cannot evaluate unit economics due to disclosure gaps

**This ranking reflects disclosure quality and data-driven confidence, not a
recommendation to buy or avoid any specific franchise.** A buyer's personal
financial situation, geographic market, risk tolerance, and operational capability
may lead to a different conclusion.
